Cold Laser Therapy

You've probably heard about using lasers in treating all kinds of human ailments. But you’ve likely heard little about using them to treat our furry best friends. Now, advancements in technology and manufacturing have made it possible to bring this cutting-edge treatment to the veterinarian’s office. The beneficial effects of laser light were first recognized almost 40 years ago. Since then, there have been thousands of studies documenting the positive effects of laser light on the various cells and tissues of the body.

What is treatment like?
Treatment involves application of the laser light through a handheld instrument. Because laser light penetrates deeply, it is not necessary to clip fur. Your pet’s eyes will be covered (either by a towel, eyewear or by focusing their attention elsewhere). People in the room will wear eye protection. The pet will feel a gentle and soothing warmth as the laser light penetrates to the affected area. Many Ipets find laser therapy to be quite soothing and relaxing. Some animals fall asleep and cats have been known to purr during treatment. The best way to describe it in human terms is as a soothing massage. The almost immediate relief of pain allows the pet to relax, reduce anxiety and tension. As the laser treatment continues and the pet further relaxes, pain will be further reduced. Treatment is tailored to meet the specific needs of each pet. Therefore, treatment times and the number of sessions required will vary. For some chronic patients, multiple joints will be treated during one laser treatment session. When appropriate, laser therapy can be used as a complementary adjunct to other treatment plans.
﻿Laser light treatment has three primary benefits: First, as the system sends packets of light, or photons, into the affected area, it stimulates the damage tissue. As the cells are stimulated, a photochemical reaction occurs, inspiring production of adenosine triphosphate (ATP), a chemical needed for cell metabolism. As ATP production increases, the cells begin to heal and reproduce in a process called cellular proliferation. Secondly, the laser light treatment stimulates the circulation of oxygen, nutrients and protein into the damaged tissues. These additional resources further support healing. This increased circulation helps to remove waste products from the damaged area, helping to reduce inflammation. Finally, laser light treatment stimulates the release of endorphins, hormones associated with a sense of well-being and pleasure. Following treatment, the injured area heals at a faster rate with less pain and inflammation. There are no known side effects of the treatment. Pet parents may see a change in activity level when the pet comes home, because of the reduction in pain and inflammation. Some pets may be more active while others may be more relaxed and calm. The reaction depends greatly on the temperament of the animal. Laser therapy for pets is sterile, pain-free, surgery-free, and drug-free. Whether your pet is rehabilitating from trauma or injury, healing from wounds, or simply aging, the laser has been shown to provide relief and speed healing.
